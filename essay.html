Reflective Essay

Essay

Throughout my work in the Digital Storytelling and Poetics minor, I’ve come to see storytelling as something that goes beyond traditional written narratives and into interactive, multimodal, and user-driven experiences. Across my portfolio, I explore how digital environments change the relationship between the author, the text, and the reader, while still connecting to older literary traditions and newer technologies. Rather than treating each project as completely separate, my work shows how digital storytelling brings together narrative, design, interactivity, and cultural context.

One of the clearest examples of this is my Twine project, Echoes of Absence: The Mystery of the Missing Children. In this project, I explore interactive fiction as a form where the reader has a more active role in shaping the story. The branching structure, along with sound and visuals, creates a more immersive experience compared to a linear text. This project helped me understand digital storytelling not just as a way to present content, but as a system where meaning is created through interaction. It also draws on ideas from Sherlock Holmes stories, remixing familiar elements into a nonlinear format. This reflects how digital storytelling builds on print traditions through adaptation and reworking. At the same time, the project highlights the connection between storytelling and games through its investigative structure and focus on decision-making.

I continued exploring storytelling as both rhetorical and multimodal in my advocacy campaign project. After analyzing digital advocacy around the Taiwan/China conflict, I looked at how strategies like framing, juxtaposition, and appeals to ethos, logos, and pathos influence audiences. I then created my own advocacy posts, designing them with specific platforms and audiences in mind. This project shows how I can move from analysis to creation, and how digital storytelling works through persuasion, visuals, and audience engagement. It also reflects how technology can amplify narratives and shape public opinion.

My Figma project for the No Arena Philadelphia campaign expands this idea even further by focusing on user experience design. While it isn’t a traditional story, the app still tells a kind of narrative by guiding users from awareness to action. I focused on accessibility, clarity, and emotional impact to show how design choices can influence user behavior. This project is also personally meaningful because of my connection to Philadelphia’s Chinatown. By creating a platform centered on community concerns, I show how digital storytelling can support and represent marginalized communities. In this case, storytelling becomes a tool for both expression and social engagement.

My analytical work, especially my essay on the digital adaptation of Nam Le’s The Boat, deepens my understanding of how digital storytelling changes the reading experience. By comparing the original story to its interactive version, I explore how elements like animation, sound, and navigation reshape the role of the reader. Using ideas from scholars like N. Katherine Hayles and Janet Murray, I argue that digital literature turns the reader into an active participant rather than a passive observer. This project shows my ability to analyze multimodal texts and connect them to larger theoretical and historical ideas, reinforcing that digital storytelling builds on earlier literary traditions rather than replacing them.

This connection to literary history also appears in my poetry project, where I experiment with constrained and found writing. By following specific rules or reusing existing text, I draw on traditions from 20th-century avant-garde literature. These techniques are similar to digital environments, where systems and constraints shape creative work. Through this project, I show how limitations can actually help create meaning, and how experimental print techniques still influence digital storytelling today.

Finally, my interactive storytelling project, The Living Library, brings all of these ideas together. In this project, users explore a library and choose different narrative paths, emphasizing nonlinear storytelling and user agency. The combination of text, visuals, sound, and interaction reflects my understanding of multimodal design, while the branching structure connects storytelling with game mechanics. Building this project with HTML, CSS, and JavaScript also highlights the technical skills I’ve developed. As I explain in my Maker’s Statement, the goal was to make the reader an active participant, reinforcing my belief that digital storytelling is shaped by interaction and collaboration.

Across all of these projects, I also think about the responsibilities that come with working in digital spaces, including the ethical use of technology and AI. While my work focuses on human-centered storytelling, I recognize the importance of being thoughtful about the tools I use and their broader impact. Overall, my portfolio shows how my understanding of digital storytelling has grown—from learning about it to actively creating and analyzing it in different forms. By combining creative work, analysis, and technical skills, I show both my ability and my perspective: that storytelling in digital spaces is not fixed or linear, but dynamic, interactive, and shaped by the technologies we use.
